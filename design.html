Position statement

My position on engineering design

This section presents the position statement in a long-form format while keeping the same visual language as the rest of the page.

Introduction

My name is Jan Kazimierczak and I’m a first-year student at the University of Toronto Engineering Science program. My interest in engineering started when I was around 13 years old with the first Falcon Heavy launch. Seeing both boosters land with so great precision was one of the most beautiful things I saw and it changed my life. Since then, I see engineering as a means of changing our world for the better. I understand engineering design to be the structured and efficient approach to solving an opportunity by modifying the world around us.

Ethical stance on engineering design + background

My ethical stance on engineering design comes from my background. I am Polish and thus I come from a Catholic background. Being Catholic, I want the wellbeing of every human being, but I believe that unfortunately in engineering projects this is not always possible. I believe this is not possible due to the complexity of human nature and human interaction. Due to this I take more of a utilitarian approach with it being closer to ethics of “minimums”, the minimums being these red lines: Cannot interfere with the freedom nor life of an innocent person. I understand these red lines will not apply in Praxis II, but as a future engineer they are important to define early on. Also, I believe my work to be my duty which requires full commitment, thus it is important for me that when I take part in engineering design, everything must be done with intention and hard work, rejecting shortcuts and sloppiness.

Practices in engineering design

Regarding my practices in engineering design, I can pinpoint 3 distinct events or projects that have shaped my approach to engineering design. These three are: Highschool science symposium project, Highschool musical stage construction and the Praxis I project.

  1. My grandfather who is an engineer himself helped me with the science symposium project for which we had to build a wind tunnel 3 meters long to perform some experiments. Having no experience, I started construction without really having any concrete plan, which meant that it would not have been as good as it could’ve. My grandfather taught me to always have control on what you are designing and building and to always proceed with caution, patience and due diligence.
  2. The second experience that shaped my approach to engineering design is the stage construction for my high school’s musical. In this case I was no longer the “Authority” in the project, as opposed to the science symposium project, but here we had no say on the decisions regarding the construction. While working on it I noticed that the decisions they made regarding the method of construction would mean that the set would probably fail when people stood on it, and it turned out that I was right. I did mention it, but because I saw myself as with no “Authority” I did not insist on it. After this experience I decided that for future engineering design projects I would raise concerns about methods or ideas to reduce potential harm done.
  3. The last experience ties closely to the stage design project. When working on the Praxis I project, it was the first time where I worked together in a team where we all were “authorities” without a single “Authority” that would present us tasks. This taught me to make compromises regarding decisions and to try to hear everyone’s perspective regarding decisions on the project.

My position on engineering design

  1. Following my grandfather’s example and advice I spend a considerable amount of time on careful planning and design so that everything works as intended on the first try. This means that my approach is systematic and cautious.
  2. While working on a project I will voice my concerns regarding decisions that I deem to be wrong or dangerous. Due diligence is crucial in engineering design so I believe we all should contribute to it.
  3. I try to consider all team members’ ideas and find compromises between them while working on a group project. This position lies closer to the “authorities” concept in praxis rather than one big “Authority”.

Approach to engineering design (Conclusion)

Having covered all these points, I can express my approach to engineering design in the following way [Figure 1]: Coming from a Catholic background I want the product of engineering design to benefit the most people possible. While working on a project I take the planning and designing stage very seriously to ensure the projects are completed with due diligence and efficiency. When encountering a problem or potential risk I raise my concern so we can work together to find a solution. And finally, when working in a group I find compromises on ideas so we can develop a good working environment to explore all ideas and solution proposals.

Selected projects

The three projects included in the portfolio

Each project reflects a different part of the position statement: compromise in team work, careful planning and performance evaluation, and systematic design through iteration.

Praxis I Design Prototyping CAD

BikePack Buddy

A practical transport solution designed to reduce rider strain by moving backpack load from the body to the bicycle system. This project connects especially well to compromise, shared decision-making, and iterative design work.
CIV102 Structures Testing Analysis

Bridge design project

A bridge project centered on material constraints, structural behavior, and performance under load. It reflects careful planning, due diligence, and the responsibility to understand whether a design is actually safe and effective.
Praxis II Mechanism design Automation Iteration

Automated screw sorting device

A compact device concept built around a specific physical task and the logic needed to perform it reliably. It represents a more systematic design problem where planning, mechanism choices, and iteration all matter.

Conclusion

Overall, this page presents engineering design the way it is described in my Praxis II statement: as work that should benefit people where possible, stay within ethical red lines, be approached carefully and systematically, and improve through both technical judgment and collaborative compromise.
